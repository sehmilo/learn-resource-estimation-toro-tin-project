Module 2

Sampling, Data and Database Integrity

⏱ 45–55 min ☰ 7 sections ⚙ Python worked example ✓ Prerequisite: Module 1

An estimate can never be better than the samples it is built from. This module is about the part of the value chain that quietly determines the accuracy of everything downstream — and it is the part most often done carelessly. We derive the theory of sampling error, then apply it to the real integrity problems sitting inside the Toro database right now.

Learning objectives

  • Quantify the fundamental sampling error using Gy's theory and explain why particle size dominates it.
  • Design appropriate sampling for an alluvial placer and for a primary hard-rock target, and explain why they differ.
  • Structure a drillhole / pit database into its four core tables and explain why the structure matters.
  • Determine bulk density correctly and quantify its direct effect on declared tonnage.
  • Build a QAQC system from standards, blanks and duplicates, and read precision and accuracy from it.
  • Audit the Toro dataset: identify what each record truly represents and composite it correctly.

2.1 The error budget: where accuracy is won and lost

Consider the scale of the extrapolation. A Toro pit bulk sample is roughly 100–200 kg. A future Toro mining operation might process several million tonnes. The sample-to-orebody mass ratio is of the order of one part in ten million or worse. Every tonne mined will have its grade predicted from that vanishingly small sampled fraction.

It is useful to think of the total error in a final block estimate as a sum of variances from independent stages:

(2.1) \[ \sigma^2_{\text{total}} \;=\; \sigma^2_{\text{sampling}} \;+\; \sigma^2_{\text{sub-sampling}} \;+\; \sigma^2_{\text{assay}} \;+\; \sigma^2_{\text{estimation}} \]
Variances add when the error sources are independent. Estimation — kriging — is only the last term. The first three are fixed before any geostatistics begins.
The order that matters

Modules 4 and 5 reduce only \(\sigma^2_{\text{estimation}}\). If the sampling and assay variances are large, no kriging plan can rescue the estimate — you would be interpolating noise with great precision. Sampling quality is logically and chronologically prior to geostatistics. This is why a resource course must spend a full module here before touching a variogram.

2.2 Gy's theory of sampling

Pierre Gy gave the mining industry a quantitative theory of sampling error. Its centrepiece is the Fundamental Sampling Error (FSE): the irreducible error that remains even with a perfectly executed sampling protocol, arising purely because ore is made of discrete, heterogeneous particles.

Gy distinguished two kinds of heterogeneity. Constitution heterogeneity is intrinsic: particles differ from one another in composition, and no amount of mixing removes it. Distribution heterogeneity is the way those particles are arranged in space, and it can be reduced by homogenisation. The FSE flows from constitution heterogeneity, which is why it is fundamental and irreducible for a given material and particle size.

For a low-grade ore sampled correctly, the variance of the FSE is:

(2.2) \[ \sigma^2_{\text{FSE}} \;=\; \left(\frac{1}{M_S}-\frac{1}{M_L}\right) f\,g\,c\,\ell\,d^{\,3} \]
Gy's fundamental sampling error equation.
Mₛ
mass of the sample taken (g)
Mₗ
mass of the lot being sampled (g); when Mₛ « Mₗ the second term vanishes
d
nominal top particle size, the 95% passing diameter (cm) — note the cube
f
shape factor, ≈ 0.5 for typical equant grains
g
granulometric (size-range) factor, ≈ 0.25 for unsorted material
c
mineralogical / composition factor (g/cm³)
ℓ
liberation factor, 0 (locked) to 1 (fully liberated)

The composition factor carries the grade dependence and, for an ore where the mineral of interest is dilute, simplifies to a revealing form:

(2.3) \[ c \;=\; \frac{1-a_L}{a_L}\big[(1-a_L)\rho_M + a_L\rho_g\big] \;\;\xrightarrow[a_L\to 0]{}\;\; \frac{\rho_M}{a_L} \]
aₗ is the fractional grade of the mineral of interest; ρₘ and ρₜ are the densities of that mineral and of the gangue. At low grade, c is inversely proportional to grade.

Two consequences of equations (2.2) and (2.3) should permanently shape how you sample.

Particle size dominates, because of the cube. The FSE variance scales with \(d^3\). Halving the top particle size by crushing cuts the sampling variance to one eighth. This is the entire reason a sampling protocol is a staged sequence of crush, split, crush, split: you cannot take a small sample of coarse material without large error, so you reduce \(d\) before you reduce mass.

Low grade plus a dense, coarse mineral is the worst case. Since \(c \propto 1/a_L\), error explodes as grade falls. Cassiterite makes this acute: it is dense (\(\rho \approx 6.9\) g/cm³), often coarse, and frequently liberates as discrete grains. A coarse cassiterite ore behaves much like a coarse-gold ore — a few heavy grains carry a disproportionate share of the metal, so whether the sample happens to catch them swings the assay violently.

Gy's theory and the nugget effect

Hold this thought for Module 4. The FSE is a variance that exists even between two samples taken at the same place. When you plot spatial continuity as a variogram, that same-location variance appears as the intercept at zero distance: the nugget effect. A large part of a tin deposit's nugget is not geology — it is Gy's fundamental sampling error, plus assay error. Improving the sampling protocol literally lowers the nugget you will fight in Module 4.

Beyond the FSE, Gy catalogued further error terms that, unlike the FSE, are avoidable by correct practice. The grouping and segregation error arises because particles segregate by density and size (heavy cassiterite settles), so a sample scooped from one spot is biased; it is controlled by taking many small increments rather than one large grab. The materialisation errors — delimitation, extraction and preparation — are the practical mistakes of defining the wrong sample shape, failing to recover it cleanly, and contaminating or losing material during preparation. These produce bias, which is far more dangerous than random error because no amount of repeat sampling reveals it.

2.3 Sampling the two Toro orebodies

The two Toro targets are sampled by fundamentally different methods, because they are fundamentally different estimation problems. Get this distinction wrong and every later module is built on sand.

The alluvial target: a volume-grade problem

Alluvial cassiterite sits in wash gravels beneath barren overburden. The economic quantity is not a percentage — it is a concentration of heavy mineral per unit volume of ground, because that is what a dredge or wash plant processes. The grade unit is therefore grams of tin (or kilograms of heavy mineral concentrate) per cubic metre.

Your field logs describe the correct method exactly. A pit is dug through overburden to the wash layer (in the Mining Pit, "the layer that host the material starts from 5.6 m down ... pit is exactly 8.6 m"); a measured bulk sample of wash gravel is taken (200 kg); it is washed and panned to a heavy-mineral concentrate (HMC); the HMC is weighed (1–2 kg recovered); and the HMC is assayed. The in-situ grade is then a chain of factors:

(2.4) \[ g_{\,\mathrm{Sn}}\;\left[\tfrac{\text{g}}{\text{m}^3}\right] \;=\;\underbrace{\frac{M_{\text{HMC}}}{M_{\text{bulk}}}}_{\text{yield}} \;\times\;\underbrace{a_{\text{Sn}}}_{\substack{\text{Sn grade}\\ \text{of HMC}}}\;\times\; \underbrace{\rho_{\text{gravel}}}_{\substack{\text{in-situ}\\ \text{density}}}\;\times\;10^{6} \]
The alluvial grade chain. Each factor is measured separately, each carries its own error, and the errors multiply.

This single equation explains why an alluvial estimate is so treacherous. The XRF assay only delivers the middle factor, \(a_{\text{Sn}}\) — the tin grade of the concentrate. The yield comes from honest weighing in the field. The density comes from a separate in-situ measurement. A 10% error in any factor is a 10% error in the answer, and they compound.

The most important sentence in this module

The Toro XRF dataset reports the grade of the panned heavy-mineral concentrate, not the grade of the ground. A sample reading "3.97% SnO₂" describes a kilogram of concentrate, not a cubic metre of gravel. To turn it into a resource grade you must multiply by the HMC yield and the bulk density, as in equation (2.4). Treating concentrate assays as in-situ grades would overstate the deposit by one to two orders of magnitude. This is the classic, fatal error in alluvial tin reporting.

The chemical evidence confirms what the samples are. Raw alluvium is quartz-rich and would assay 70–90% SiO₂. The Toro field samples average roughly 32% SiO₂ and 29% Fe₂O₃ — these are heavy-mineral concentrates, the product of panning, exactly as the field logs describe.

The primary target: a grade-tonnage problem

The primary greisen and pegmatite target is sampled like any hard-rock deposit. The grade unit is % Sn (or g/t) of rock in place. The methods:

MethodWhat it givesUse at Toro
Diamond core drillingIntact core: grade, true width, structure, density, geotechnical dataDefinitive primary resource drilling on the LV 029 cupola
Reverse-circulation (RC) drillingRock chips: fast, cheap grade, no structureGrade infill once geometry is known
Channel / chip samplingContinuous chip across an exposed widthGreisen veins and pegmatites in pits and outcrop
Pitting / trenchingNear-surface 3D control Eluvial cover and weathered cupola

Two cautions specific to the primary target. First, true width: a grade applies to a true thickness measured perpendicular to the mineralised structure, not to the down-hole interval. Drilling oblique to a vein inflates the apparent width and must be corrected by the intersection geometry. Second, refractory tin chemistry: cassiterite resists aqua-regia digestion, so an under-spec assay simply fails to dissolve the tin and reports a low grade. Toro analyses must specify four-acid digestion or, better, lithium-borate fusion XRF — which is exactly what the ANRML26-137 laboratory used (method XRF17, lithium borate fusion). That choice was correct.

2.4 The drillhole and pit database

Estimation software does not consume field notebooks. It consumes a relational database with a strict structure. Whether the data come from drillholes or pits, four core tables carry it, linked by a unique hole or pit identifier.

TableKey fieldsPurpose
CollarHoleID, Easting, Northing, RL, total depth Where each hole or pit starts
SurveyHoleID, depth, azimuth, dip The path of the hole in 3D (trivial for vertical pits)
AssayHoleID, from, to, Sn, Nb, Ta ... Grade over each sampled interval
Lithology / geologyHoleID, from, to, rock code, weathering, alterationThe geological log that defines domains

The non-negotiable rules: every interval is defined by a from and a to depth; intervals never overlap and never leave gaps; every HoleID in the assay table must exist in the collar table; coordinates use one declared system (for Toro, WGS84 UTM Zone 32N, as your roadmap specifies). The desurvey step combines collar and survey to compute the true 3D coordinate of every assay interval — the point that later carries a grade into the variogram and the kriging system.

Toro's priority drilling collars

Your Priority Drilling Points file is already a well-formed collar table in embryo: 13 collars (DBD, DKD, DKK series) with Easting, Northing, longitude and latitude at Bishiwai, Yadabongo and Kadade. To become a resource database it now needs survey, assay and lithology tables populated against those same HoleIDs — and a defensible QAQC stream running through the assays.

2.5 Bulk density — the silent half of every tonne

Tonnage is volume multiplied by density. Geologists lavish attention on grade and routinely under-sample density, yet the two enter the contained-metal calculation with exactly equal weight.

(2.5) \[ T \;=\; V \times \rho \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad \frac{\Delta T}{T} \;=\; \frac{\Delta \rho}{\rho} \]
A proportional error in density is a proportional error in tonnage, and therefore in contained metal, one-for-one.

If Toro adopts a density of 1.8 t/m³ for the wash gravel when the true figure is 2.0, every tonnage and every contained-tin figure in the resource statement is understated by 10% — a material misstatement under any code. Density must be measured, by domain and by weathering state, not assumed.

MethodBest forPitfall
Water displacement (Archimedes) on coreCompetent, non-porous primary rockPorous or fractured samples absorb water and over-read density
Wax-coating then displacementPorous or friable samplesCoating must be complete; wax density corrected for
In-situ pit / sand replacementLoose alluvial gravel that cannot be coredLabour-intensive; needs careful volume measurement
Down-hole geophysical (gamma-gamma)Continuous density loggingNeeds calibration against physical samples

For the Toro alluvial target the wash gravel is unconsolidated, so core displacement is impossible: an in-situ method (a measured excavation, weigh the material removed, survey the void volume) is required, and it must record the natural moisture state, since the codes require tonnage to be reported on a declared dry or wet basis. For the primary granite, fresh rock is near 2.6–2.7 t/m³, but the weathered cupola that hosts near-surface tin can be far lower, so density must be tied to the weathering profile in the lithology log.

2.6 QAQC: deciding whether the database can be trusted

Quality Assurance and Quality Control is the evidence system that lets a Competent Person certify the assay database. Without it, an estimate is uninsurable: you cannot defend numbers you cannot prove. QAQC separates two distinct properties of the data.

Accuracy and precision

Accuracy is freedom from bias — do the assays sit on the true value on average? Precision is repeatability — how widely do repeat measurements of the same material scatter? They are independent. Data can be precise but biased (consistently 15% low), or accurate but imprecise (right on average, wild scatter). A resource needs both, and they are demonstrated by three different control instruments.

InstrumentTestsHowTypical rate
Certified Reference Material (standard)Accuracy A pulp of known, certified grade inserted blind into the batch; must return within ±2 standard deviations1 in 20
BlankContaminationBarren material inserted after high-grade samples; must return near detection limit 1 in 20
DuplicatePrecisionA second sample of the same material assayed independently; the pair scatter measures repeatability1 in 20

Standards are read on a control chart: the assayed value of the CRM plotted in batch order, with the certified mean and the ±2 and ±3 standard-deviation lines drawn. A point beyond ±3σ, or a run of points on one side of the mean, signals a batch that must be investigated and probably re-assayed.

Duplicates come in a hierarchy, and the hierarchy is informative. A field (or twin) duplicate repeats the whole sampling act and captures the total error, FSE included. A coarse (preparation) duplicate is split after crushing and isolates preparation error. A pulp duplicate is a second assay of the same final powder and isolates pure analytical error. Precision is read from the pair scatter, commonly as the relative difference:

(2.6) \[ \mathrm{RD} \;=\; \frac{|x_1-x_2|}{\tfrac{1}{2}(x_1+x_2)}\times 100\% \]
Relative difference of a duplicate pair. Plotting many pairs, well-behaved precision keeps most pairs within a tolerance band (often 20–30% for a base-metal assay; wider for nuggety minerals).
What Toro's data cannot yet tell you

The Toro geochemical file contains several paired records at identical coordinates — for example DBP-005A and DBP-005B, DBP-029A and DBP-029B′, DBP-033A and DBP-033A′. The within-pair spread is large: DBP-033's pair reads 7.59% and 2.28% SnO₂ — a relative difference above 100%. But you cannot currently tell why. Those suffixes might mean two stratigraphic layers in the pit (real vertical variability, which is geology), or a re-split of one sample (poor precision, which is a QAQC failure). The reconnaissance sampling was not designed with a QAQC structure, so the two explanations are confounded and cannot be separated. That, in one example, is why a designed QAQC protocol — with deliberately labelled, blind, randomly inserted duplicates — must run through the next Toro campaign from the first sample.

2.7 Worked example: auditing the Toro database

Apply the module. The file Motimose_GeoChemData.csv holds 40 XRF records. Before a single statistic is computed, each record must be classified by what it physically represents — the discipline of section 2.3.

Some records are not in-situ samples at all. Their identifiers carry mineral-separation tags (Nb, Fe, Zr, SnO2+SiO2). These are laboratory products: heavy-mineral fractions the lab separated out of one field sample. The record DBP-010 SnO2+SiO2-2 assays 65.4% SnO₂ — that is a lab-made cassiterite concentrate, not a grade that exists anywhere in the ground. Including it in a grade statistic would be meaningless. It must be filtered out.

Python classify every lab record by what it represents
import pandas as pd, numpy as np

geo = pd.read_csv("Motimose_GeoChemData.csv")

# IDs carrying a mineral-separation tag are laboratory heavy-mineral
# fractions, NOT in-situ field samples. They must not enter grade stats.
sep_tags = ["Nb", "Fe", "Zr", "SnO2+SiO2", "FeB", "SnO2 (2)"]

def is_separation(sid):
    s = str(sid)
    return any(t in s for t in sep_tags) and len(s.split()) > 1

geo["record_type"] = np.where(
    geo["Lab_Sample_ID"].apply(is_separation),
    "lab_separation", "field_sample")

print(geo["record_type"].value_counts())
# field_sample      32
# lab_separation     8

Eight of the 40 records are laboratory fractions; 32 are genuine field samples. Next, those 32 still over-represent some pits, because a pit logged with two layers (an A and a B sample) appears twice. For a spatial estimate each location must contribute one grade, or the variogram and the kriging system will see false zero-distance pairs. We composite the layer samples to one grade per pit.

Python composite layer splits to one grade per pit location
fs = geo[geo.record_type == "field_sample"].copy()

comp = (fs.groupby("Field_Pit_ID")
          .agg(Location =("Location",  "first"),
               Latitude =("Latitude",  "first"),
               Longitude=("Longitude", "first"),
               n_splits =("SnO2",      "size"),
               SnO2     =("SnO2",      "mean"))   # length-weight if logged
          .reset_index())

print(len(fs), "field samples ->", len(comp), "pit locations")
# 32 field samples -> 26 pit locations
The audit result

The raw "40-sample" dataset resolves into 26 pit locations with a defensible in-situ concentrate grade. That number — not 40 — is the real spatial dataset that Modules 3, 4 and 5 work with. Notice that proper compositing should be length-weighted (a 2 m layer counts more than a 0.5 m layer); the simple mean above is used only because the current field logs do not record consistent layer thicknesses. Recording sampled thickness on every future pit is therefore not bookkeeping — it is a prerequisite for a correct composite.

Check your understanding: A Toro bulk sample of wash gravel returns 4% SnO₂ from the laboratory. Why can this number not be entered into a resource model as a block grade?
Because SnO₂ must first be converted to Sn metal.
Because XRF is not accurate enough for tin.
Because it is the grade of the panned concentrate; the in-situ grade also needs the HMC yield and the bulk density of the gravel.
Because 4% is below a typical tin cut-off.
The assay describes the heavy-mineral concentrate, not the ground. The in-situ grade is the chain of equation (2.4): yield (HMC mass per bulk mass) multiplied by the concentrate grade multiplied by in-situ bulk density. Skipping the yield and density factors would overstate the deposit by one to two orders of magnitude. The SnO₂-to-Sn conversion is also needed, but it is a fixed 0.79 factor, not the reason the raw number is unusable.
